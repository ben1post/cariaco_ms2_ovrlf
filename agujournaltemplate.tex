\begin{abstract}

Phytoplankton communities are integral to oceanic biogeochemical cycles and are sensitive indicators of climate-driven environmental variability.
Long-term time series capture this variability, allowing us to unravel the effects of environmental change on local communities. This study investigates changes in the phytoplankton community of the Cariaco Basin, off the coast of Venezuela, from 1996 to 2016, using monthly water column observations integrated with climate indices and meteorological data. To investigate impacts of larger-scale climate variability, we considered the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO) index and the Multivariate El Niño Southern Oscillation index (MEI v.2), both linked to variations in local weather and upwelling in the Cariaco Basin.
Cluster analysis identified two distinct community states separated by a shift from high to low upwelling conditions after 2003. In 2014, environmental conditions shifted back toward a stronger upwelling regime, and community composition began transitioning toward the earlier state (pre-2004). No significant differences in genus richness or Shannon diversity were observed between the two periods. The low upwelling period, however, was characterized by significantly higher community evenness and lower species turnover.
To quantify effects of climate and bottom-up drivers on phytoplankton community changes, we employed gradient forest analysis and found the AMO index was the strongest predictor of changes in the community, closely followed by in situ variables related to upwelling (sea surface temperature and nitrate concentration) and the MEI v.2. 
Our study emphasizes the impact of long-term climatic oscillations, such as the AMO, on the phytoplankton community of a tropical coastal ecosystem through modulation of the upwelling system. The indications of a transition toward pre-2004 conditions, which began to appear in 2014, suggest a cyclic behavior of the ecosystem under decadal-scale climate forcing.
\end{abstract}

% Topic Overview
Phytoplankton are photosynthesizing unicellular microorganisms that form the foundation of the ocean food web. Since they respond quickly to environmental changes, studying them can show how marine ecosystems are affected by climate.
% Paper Overview
This study investigated how phytoplankton communities in the Cariaco Basin, off the coast of Venezuela, changed from 1996 to 2016. We compared microscopy cell counts and monthly water samples with weather data and two major climate indicators: the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO) index and the Multivariate El Niño Southern Oscillation index (MEI v.2), both linked to local weather and currents.
% Findings Summary
The results showed a major shift in the phytoplankton community in 2004, linked to a decrease in upwelling, conditions showing signs of reversal in 2014, transitioning back toward the pre-2004 state. Interestingly, community diversity declined between 1998 and 2009 independently of changes in the upwelling regime. Further analysis showed that the AMO index, along with sea surface temperature, MEI v.2 and nitrate concentrations in the water column, were the strongest predictors of changes in the phytoplankton community.

% Key Takeaways
These findings contribute to a broader understanding of how climate variability shapes marine phytoplankton communities and underscore the importance of sustained, long-term observations in detecting and interpreting ecological responses to climate change.

% Phytoplankton is important
Phytoplankton provides the foundation for marine food webs and plays crucial roles in biogeochemical processes \cite{falkowski_biogeochemical_1998}. Their short generation times and high taxonomic and functional group diversity allow phytoplankton communities to respond relatively quickly to environmental changes, making them suitable indicators of ecosystem state and dynamics \cite{alvarez-cobelas_what_1998, barton_anthropogenic_2016, di_cavalho_temporal_2023}. Long-term time series of biological observations are crucial for understanding how phytoplankton communities respond to both short- and long-term changes in the physical environment and climate \cite{carstensen_need_2014, henson_observing_2016}. Such time series are rare, particularly in the tropics \cite{clarke_does_2017}. 

A tropical coastal location that has been intensively studied in the last few decades is the Cariaco Basin, where a wide range of physical and biological parameters were collected consistently between 1995 and 2017 \cite{muller-karger_scientific_2019}. The Cariaco Basin, located off the coast of Venezuela in the southern Caribbean Sea, is a highly productive ecosystem within one of the largest anoxic basins in the world's oceans \cite{edgcomb_accessing_2011}. The Cariaco Basin is characterized by a marked seasonal cycle of upwelling, which is driven by easterly trade winds, with the highest levels of primary production observed from December through May when the Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ) moves southward. A smaller wind-driven secondary upwelling event often occurs between June and August \cite{mullerkarger_annual_2001, astor_seasonal_2003}. During the upwelling periods, the supply of deeper nutrient-rich waters supports high biological productivity, and the phytoplankton community is dominated by diatoms \cite{romero_seasonal_2009}, with dinoflagellates, coccolithophorids, and nanoplankton also contributing. In the low-wind, rainy season (September–November), the Cariaco Basin exhibits oligotrophic conditions, and the fraction of large phytoplankton cells is reduced. However, nano- and picophytoplankton cells are present in relatively high numbers throughout the year \cite{lorenzoni_characterization_2015}.    

% PARAGRAPH THREE - CHANGES IN CARIACO
Since the inception of the time series in 1995, the ecosystem of the Cariaco Basin has undergone marked changes. \citeA{taylor_ecosystem_2012} analyzed the time series data up to 2010 and found decadal trends of increasing sea surface temperature (SST), coinciding with a decrease in upwelling and phytoplankton bloom intensities, which the authors attributed to effects of global climate change. During the initial phase of the time series (1996–2003), the Cariaco Basin underwent consistent and pronounced seasonal upwelling cycles, driven by strong trade winds during the primary upwelling season (December–May) \cite{mullerkarger_annual_2001, astor_seasonal_2003}. After 2004, a significant reduction in the strength of trade winds during the primary upwelling period caused a regime change in the physical and biogeochemical system \cite{taylor_ecosystem_2012}. This strongly affected the phytoplankton community and the entire ecosystem. Microscopy cell counts indicated a drastic reduction of large phytoplankton, particularly diatoms \cite{pinckney_phytoplankton_2015}. In general, a shift was observed in the phytoplankton community toward smaller cell sizes, along with increased abundances of coccolithophores, cryptophytes, and other phytoflagellates, groups commonly found in stratified water columns \cite{lorenzoni_characterization_2015}. An analysis of pigment data indicated a deepening of the chlorophyll maximum, an increase in pigment diversity, and a reduction in the seasonal variability of community composition \cite{pinckney_phytoplankton_2015}. 

% PARAGRAPH FOUR - EXPLANATION OF CHANGES // OPEN QUESTIONS!!
The shift in phytoplankton community composition associated with increased temperature and reduced upwelling intensity from 2004 to 2013 did not appear to represent a permanent regime change. \linelabel{R2:C8:1} Hydrographic data from 2014 to 2017, the final years of the CARIACO (CArbon Retention In A Colored Ocean) time series, indicated the beginning of a return to strong upwelling conditions, while aggregated microscopy cell counts from 2014 and 2015 showed increased abundances of diatoms and dinoflagellates \cite{muller-karger_scientific_2019}. Although the impact of large-scale climatic processes on the observed shifts in phytoplankton community composition remains unclear, we hypothesize that these changes were driven by decadal-scale changes in climatic factors. The Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO) has emerged as an important factor influencing both the regional climate system and north Atlantic ecosystems \cite{nye_ecosystem_2014}. \linelabel{R2:C7} A negative AMO phase has been linked to a southward shift in the ITCZ (\cite{knight_climate_2006}), which strengthens trade winds over the southern Caribbean and intensifies wind-driven upwelling in the Cariaco Basin (\cite{taylor_ecosystem_2012}). At the regional scale, AMO oscillations are strongly correlated with Caribbean climate variability, with negative phases associated with drier conditions (\cite{stephenson_changes_2014}). The El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO) is teleconnected to the Caribbean Sea, given that during ENSO (indicated by a positive MEI v.2 index) sea surface temperatures increase and trade winds blow more to the north-northwest \cite{enfield_tropical_1997}. However, using the time series available only until 2010, \citeA{taylor_ecosystem_2012} found no significant correlation between MEI v.2 and in situ observations and did not test for correlations with the AMO index.

% What drives the changes??? Bottom-up/ Top-down ... CLIMATE 
\linelabel{R2:C9} \citeA{taylor_ecosystem_2012} noted that the observed trends of increasing SST, reduced upwelling intensity, and reduced chlorophyll \textit{a} between 1996 and 2010 could not be clearly identified as ``unidirectional trends driven by anthropogenic climate change or whether they simply reflect low-frequency natural cycles, such as those driving the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation''. To our knowledge, all analyses of phytoplankton community diversity in the Cariaco Basin do not include time series data beyond 2012, and no detailed investigations have yet been conducted on changes in diversity based on microscopy cell counts. Given these knowledge gaps, we aim to investigate 1) changes in the depth distribution of phytoplankton biomass and community diversity over the period from 1996 to 2016, 2) changes in the structure of the phytoplankton community and potential connections with shifts observed in physical and biogeochemical variables, and 3) potential connections between long-term climatic oscillations (ENSO MEI v.2 and AMO) and changes in the phytoplankton community. Our analysis focuses exclusively on bottom-up drivers of community changes because these data are available throughout the entire time series and at a monthly resolution. Our study builds on and expands the work of \citeA{taylor_ecosystem_2012} and \citeA{pinckney_phytoplankton_2015} with a more detailed analysis of biodiversity aspects using all data available from the CARIACO time series up to 2016. In doing so, we aim to provide a more comprehensive assessment of long-term phytoplankton community dynamics in the Cariaco Basin, offering new insights into the local ecological responses to environmental variability across decadal scales.

\subsection{Study Site}
    %Talk about the Basin shortly, then about the time series
    The Cariaco Ocean Time Series Program was established to explore the relationship between ocean surface processes and the sinking flux of particulate carbon. The Cariaco Basin (Figure~\ref{fig:map}) is a large (approximately 160 km long and 70 km wide) and deep (approximately 1,400 m) basin situated on the northeastern Venezuelan continental shelf, with a sill to the north running almost parallel to the coastline at a mean depth of around \qty{100}{m}. \linelabel{R1:C5:start} This area is relatively productive (mean integrated primary production across the entire time series of \qty{485 \pm 345}{g~C~m^{-2}~y^{-1}}), with primary production and vertical particulate organic matter fluxes exceeding those observed at the Bermuda Atlantic Time-series Study (BATS) and Hawaii Ocean Time-series (HOT) sites. \linelabel{R1:C5:end} This high productivity, combined with reduced ventilation across the sill, \linelabel{R1:C6} resulted in the formation of anoxic conditions below a depth of \qty{\sim 275}{m}.

\subsection{In-situ biological and environmental data}
    The CARIACO Ocean Time Series Program collected biological, chemical and physical data, which we used here to examine the connections between environmental and ecological change in the region. Samples were collected on monthly research cruises at a mooring station located in the eastern Cariaco Basin (\ang{10.50}N, \ang{64.67}W) \cite{muller-karger_scientific_2019}.
    The CARIACO field program consisted of a monthly core oceanographic cruise with Niskin bottle and CTD deployments, along with other deployments and samples not utilized in this work. Details about sampling procedures, analytical methods, data quality control, and inter-calibration procedures have already been described by \citeA{mullerkarger_annual_2001, thunell_particulate_2007, astor_yrene_m_handbook_2013, astor_interannual_2013, astor_sintesis_2014}. 
    
    All samples were collected using a 12-bottle rosette system equipped with a CTD (SeaBird, model SBE 25) \cite{astor_yrene_m_handbook_2013}. Water samples for chlorophyll \textit{a} and nutrient analysis were taken monthly during the first cast of each cruise. These samples were collected at eight standard depths within the upper 100 meters (1, 7, 15, 25, 35, 55, 75, and 100 m). 

    Between late 1995 and early 1998, nutrient data, including nitrate, phosphate, and silicate, were provided by William Senior (Universidad de Oriente, Cumana, Venezuela) and from mid-1997 to present by Dr. K. Fanning (University of South Florida, St. Petersburg, USA). \linelabel{R1:C7} There is an overlap of eight nutrient samples analyzed by both laboratories \cite{taylor_ecosystem_2012}. We used a merged dataset for all nutrients, in which these eight coincidental monthly measurements are averaged.
       
    For the identification of phytoplankton species by microscopy, \qty{500}{\milli\liter} seawater samples were collected at depths of 1, 7, 15, 25, 35, 55, 75, and 100 m in HDPE bottles and preserved with a \qty{5}{\%} (final concentration) formalin solution neutralized with sodium tetraborate. Species counts were performed at the Universidad de Oriente, Venezuela, following the Utermöhl method \cite{hasle_inverted_1978}, using \qty{100}{\milli\liter} sedimentation chambers with a 48-hour settling period. A light microscope with 100x magnification was used, allowing only larger cells (microphytoplankton, i.e. \textgreater \qty{20}{\micro\meter}) to be clearly identified \cite{astor_yrene_m_handbook_2013}.

    Quality control of the microscopy data was performed by assembling the taxonomic identifications made across all cruises, removing observations that were not identified at the species or genus level, correcting spelling variations, and grouping synonyms using taxonomic information from the World Register of Marine Species (WoRMS, \url{marinespecies.org}). A total of 482 phytoplankton species were observed over the course of the time series, which were grouped into 223 phytoplankton genera. The identified genera were further grouped into functional groups based on their taxonomic classification in WoRMS (see Table \ref{tab:sup:GenusFuncGroups} in Appendix).
    
    All in situ data, including the Niskin bottle \cite{muller-karger_time-series_2019}, CTD \cite{muller-karger_time_2019}, and phytoplankton taxonomy \cite{troccoli_time-series_2019}, were obtained from the Biological and Chemical Oceanography Data Management Office (BCO-DMO). 
    From the CARIACO Time Series data, we used only those variables that cover the entire length of the time series without large gaps (no more than six months) in coverage. Monthly sampling coverage varied across the time series, with more complete coverage during 1996–2013 and sparser observations in 2014–2016. A complete overview of missing monthly data for all variables is provided in supplementary Table~\ref{tab:sup:missing-data}.

\subsection{Meteorological and climate data}
    %from Taylor et al. 2012
    \linelabel{R2:C10} In addition to the in situ data, we also examined meteorological and climatic data that are known to influence ocean conditions and marine ecosystems. Zonal wind (\textit{u}, easterly Trade Winds) across the southern Caribbean Sea drives nearshore upwelling of nutrient-rich waters \cite{rueda-roa_southern_2013}. We extracted the variable u10, the \qty{10}{\meter} eastward component of wind speed from the monthly averaged ECMWF Reanalysis v5 (ERA5) climate model and data reanalysis \cite{hersbach_era5_2023} in the grid cell closest to the CARIACO time series sampling location (\ang{10.4}N, \ang{64.8}W, 31 km grid size). \linelabel{R2:C12} To signify the easterly wind, we used the negative of the u10 value in our analysis such that positive values correspond to stronger easterly (westward-blowing) winds and, for simplicity, we refer to this variable as ``wind speed'' throughout the article. The data for precipitation are also extracted from the ERA5 dataset for the aforementioned grid cell. Precipitation is an estimate of total precipitation, comprising all water that falls on Earth's surface as rain or snow. These variables were all available at monthly resolution across the entire time series. We used these variables, in addition to the climate indices, in the analysis of annual z-scores, in the NMDS clustering analysis to explain variance in the monthly community data, and as predictors of community change in the gradient forest analysis.
    
    In addition to the wind data, we examined two climatic indices, each of which provides information on the basin-scale climatic conditions and, consequently, the broad-scale forcing of marine ecosystems. The monthly Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO) index (10-year low-pass) is calculated using area-averaged, detrended low-pass filtered North Atlantic SST anomalies (\ang{0}N-\ang{60}N) \cite{trenberth_climate_2023}. 
    The bi-monthly multivariate El Niño/Southern Oscillation (ENSO) index (MEI v.2) is defined as the time series of the leading combined Empirical Orthogonal Function (EOF) of five variables (sea level pressure, SST, zonal and meridional components of surface wind, and outgoing long-wave radiation) over the tropical Pacific basin (\ang{30}S-\ang{30}N and \ang{100}E-\ang{70}W) \cite{noaa_physical_sciences_laboratory_multivariate_2025}. In our analysis, the bi-monthly values were aligned with the tail month (i.e., December–January was assigned to January).


    \subsubsection{Data processing}
    Data were processed and analyzed using the R programming language (version 4.3.3) \cite{r_core_team_r_2024} with data pipelines created using ``tidyverse'' packages \cite{wickham_welcome_2019}. \linelabel{R2:C13:start} Depth-discrete measurements were collected at eight standard depths (1, 7, 15, 25, 35, 55, 75, and 100~m). These measurements were interpolated to a regular 1~m depth grid using the ``oce'' package \cite{kelley_oce_2023} with the adaptive ``unesco'' algorithm \cite{johnson_world_2006}. Profiles with more than one missing discrete depth sample were excluded from analysis. Phytoplankton cell counts and chlorophyll~\textit{a} were depth-integrated over the upper 100~m to obtain total abundance and biomass per water column. Nutrient concentrations were depth-averaged over the upper 100~m. For Figure~\ref{fig:divts}, values were additionally calculated for four depth intervals (0-25, 25-50, 50-75, and 75-100~m). Sea surface temperature (SST) was calculated from CTD temperature measurements averaged over the upper 10~m, and the depth of the 21°C isotherm was determined as the shallowest depth below 6~m where interpolated temperature fell below 21°C. For phytoplankton microscopy data, individually identified taxonomic units (species or genus) were interpolated, depth-integrated, and then grouped by genus. \linelabel{R2:C13:end} 
    Three seasons were defined for additional analyses following \cite{muller-karger_scientific_2019}: primary upwelling (December–May), secondary upwelling (June–August),  and rainy season (September–November).
    



\subsection{Data analysis}   
 
    Our analysis aimed to (1) identify temporal structure in phytoplankton community composition and diversity, and (2) assess potential bottom-up drivers of observed changes.
    
    \linelabel{R1:C12:start} To provide an overview of the available data and to highlight interannual trends in the time series data, we calculated annual anomalies for relevant variables as normalized z-scores, $z = (x\,-\,\mu)\,/\,\sigma$, where $x$ is the yearly mean, $\mu$ is the mean, and $\sigma$ is the standard deviation of all yearly means. The analyzed time series spans 1996–2016. Data from 1995 and 2017 were excluded due to insufficient coverage (see Section~2.4.3 for details on missing data handling). \linelabel{R1:C12:end}
    \linelabel{R2:C16} Annual cell counts per functional group were calculated by first interpolating and integrating depth-discrete measurements over the upper 100~m for each taxon, then summing by functional group per month, and finally averaging monthly totals per year. \linelabel{R1:C3} Nanoflagellates were not identified beyond this broad category and are therefore included only in Figure~\ref{fig:zscore} and excluded from all other analyses.
    To characterize community structure, we calculated three diversity metrics for each monthly sample: genus richness (number of genera present), Shannon diversity ($H' = -\sum_i [p_i \ln(p_i)]$), and Pielou's evenness ($J' = H'/\ln(S)$), where $p_i$ is the proportion of total cells belonging to genus $i$ and $S$ is the total number of genera. Genus-level identification, while coarser than species-level, provides a metric robust to variation in sampling effort \cite{ptacnik_diversity_2008, de_oliveira_jr_higher_2020}. Annual diversity values were calculated as means of monthly observations.

    \linelabel{R:ClarifyAggregation} Analyses in this study used two temporal resolutions. Annually aggregated data were used for: z-score anomaly calculations (Figure~\ref{fig:zscore}), hierarchical clustering based on presence-absence (Figure~\ref{fig:clustering}a), species turnover calculations (Figure~\ref{fig:clustering}b), and depth-resolved time series (Figure~\ref{fig:divts}). Monthly resolution data were used for: NMDS ordination (Figure~\ref{fig:clustering}c), density distribution comparisons between clusters (Figure~\ref{fig:clustcomp}), and gradient forest analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:GF}). The depth-resolved time series (Figure~\ref{fig:divts}) differs from other analyses in that data were integrated over four depth intervals (0–25, 25–50, 50–75, and 75–100~m) rather than the full 0–100~m range.
      
    \subsubsection{Analysis of Phytoplankton Community Structure}
        To examine temporal changes in the phytoplankton community structure in the Cariaco Basin, we applied complete linkage hierarchical clustering using the ``hclust'' function in R \cite{r_core_team_r_2024} on the binary Jaccard distance matrix. 
        This analysis was based on presence-absence data of observed genera, over the upper 100 meters of the water column and aggregated annually. For years with incomplete monthly coverage, annual presence-absence was determined from all available months. The binary (presence-absence) approach is relatively robust to missing observations, as a genus needs only to be detected once to be recorded as present. By using binary data and temporal aggregation, we aimed to reduce the influence of extreme values in the cell counts and focus on broad compositional shifts in the community. To assess the robustness of the clustering results, we conducted a sensitivity analysis comparing four combinations of distance metrics (Jaccard on presence-absence data, Bray-Curtis on cube-root transformed abundances) and linkage methods (complete linkage and Ward's method). Results are presented in supplementary Figure~\ref{fig:sup:annual_clust_comp}.
        The hierarchical clustering identified two main clusters: Cluster~1, comprising 1996–2003 and 2014–2016, and Cluster~2, comprising the intervening years 2004–2013. Because Cluster~1 spans two separate periods, we subdivided it into Early Cluster~1 (1996–2003) and Late Cluster~1 (2014–2016) to test whether the final years of the time series represent a return to pre-2004 conditions.

        To check whether annual aggregation smoothed out seasonal signals, we performed non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) ordination using individual monthly cell counts (n=220), cube-root transformed to reduce the influence of highly abundant taxa. NMDS was performed using ``metaMDS'' from the ``vegan'' package \cite{oksanen_vegan_2024} with a Bray-Curtis distance matrix and two-dimensional ordination. Environmental vectors were fitted onto the ordination using ``envfit'' with 999 permutations. In contrast to the annual clustering, the NMDS ordination retains the monthly resolution, including observations from 2016, and takes abundance of genera into account.

        We compared environmental conditions and diversity metrics among the three cluster periods using density distribution plots of monthly measurements, generated with the ``ggpubr'' package \cite{kassambara_ggpubr_2023}. 
        Statistical differences among clusters were assessed using two-sided Wilcoxon rank-sum tests, a non-parametric method appropriate for comparing distributions without assuming normality. We performed pairwise comparisons between Early Cluster~1 (1996--2003), Cluster~2 (2004--2013), and Late Cluster~1 (2014--2016), as well as a pooled comparison combining Early and Late Cluster~1 against Cluster~2. This yielded 80 tests (20 variables $\times$ 4 comparisons), so p-values were adjusted for multiple comparisons using the Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR) procedure \cite{benjamini_controlling_1995}. We note that monthly oceanographic measurements typically exhibit temporal autocorrelation, which violates the assumption of independent observations. Consequently, these tests should be interpreted as descriptive comparisons of cluster distributions. Results are reported in Table~\ref{tab:ClustCompWilcox}.
        
        To quantify the relative contributions of interannual versus seasonal variation to community structure, we performed permutational multivariate analysis of variance (PERMANOVA) using the ``adonis2'' function from the ``vegan'' package \cite{oksanen_vegan_2024} with 999 permutations. We tested temporal clusters (Early Cluster~1, Cluster~2, Late Cluster~1) and seasons (primary upwelling, secondary upwelling, rainy season) as explanatory factors for community composition based on the Bray-Curtis distance matrix of cube-root transformed monthly abundances (see Figure~\ref{fig:sup:nmds_season} and Table~\ref{tab:sup:permanova_season_cluster} in the supplementary material).


    
    \subsubsection{Assessing Environmental Drivers of Phytoplankton Dynamics}
        To quantify the effects of bottom-up drivers on shifts in the phytoplankton community of the Cariaco Basin, we employed a gradient forest analysis using the ``gradientForest'' R package \cite{ellis_gradient_2012}. Gradient forest is a community-level extension of random forest analysis that combines regression trees calculated for indicator variables to assess turnover in indicator variables across predictor variables \cite{pitcher_example_2012, large_critical_2015, tam_comparing_2017}. Gradient forest calculates a goodness-of-fit statistic for each predictor variable and indicator variable, then averages these to determine the overall importance of each predictor. The regression tree split values also identify the ranges of predictors in which significant changes occur in the indicator variables, signaling a threshold for phytoplankton community changes. Cumulative plots of the predictive importance of each predictor variable can reveal thresholds for ecosystem drivers, as steep increases in cumulative importance signal a threshold for community turnover in the phytoplankton community \cite{tam_comparing_2017}. 

        The response variables were monthly genus-level abundances integrated over the upper \qty{100}{\meter}, limited to genera counted more than 10 times over the entire time series (89 genera retained). Abundances were log(1+x) transformed to normalize their highly skewed distributions. The predictor variables were the AMO and MEI v.2 indices, wind speed, precipitation, SST, \qty{21}{\celsius} isotherm depth, salinity, and nutrient concentrations ($\mathrm{[NO_3]}$, $\mathrm{[PO_4]}$, and $\mathrm{[SiO_4]}$; Table~\ref{tab:DataSources}). These variables represent potential bottom-up drivers of community change, ranging from direct influences (e.g., nutrients and isotherm depth) to indirect climatic forcing (e.g., AMO and MEI v.2). Predictor variables were depth-averaged over the upper \qty{100}{\meter}. \linelabel{R2:C18} Because gradient forest identifies splits based on relative changes across the predictor range, the choice between depth-averaged and depth-integrated values does not affect results. After excluding months with missing data, the final dataset comprised 193 complete monthly observations (out of 240 possible months from 1996–2016).
        
        The environmental variables from ocean time series tend to be strongly correlated, which the gradient forest methodology accounts for by implementing a conditional permutation of the correlated predictors \cite{ellis_gradient_2012}. Gradient forest runs were performed with 1500 bootstrapped trees and a correlation threshold of 0.5.
        We show a model run without any time lags in the supplementary material (Figure \ref{fig:sup:GFoutput_nolags}). To consider the temporal lag in effect, particularly of climate indices, we tested for an improved predictive power of time lags of up to 12 months and 2-5 years for all climate variables, as well as 3-month lags for in situ measurements in exploratory runs. We chose time lags that scored highest in the goodness-of-fit statistics for the final model run (Table \ref{tab:sup:GFlagtests} in Appendix).
        To assess whether the dominant predictors differed among seasons, we additionally ran gradient forest models separately for primary upwelling (n=104), secondary upwelling (n=47), and rainy season (n=42) observations. Given the limited sample sizes for the latter two seasons, we interpret these results as exploratory (Figure~\ref{fig:sup:GF_seasonal}).
        All the codes and data used in this analysis and used to create the plots are publicly available on GitHub at \url{https://github.com/ben1post/PhytoCariaco} \cite{post_ben1postphytocariaco_2025}.

    \subsubsection{Missing data}
        \linelabel{R:misdat} The CARIACO time series had variable monthly coverage, particularly in the final years when funding constraints reduced sampling frequency. Data from 1995 (n=2) and 2017 (n=1) were excluded from all analyses due to insufficient temporal coverage and because these observations could not be reliably assigned to any community cluster. The analyzed time series thus spans 1996–2016. A complete overview of missing months for all variables is provided in supplementary Table~\ref{tab:sup:missing-data}.
    
        We addressed data gaps in several ways depending on the analysis. For analyses requiring robust annual means (z-scores in Figure~\ref{fig:zscore} and annual means in Figure~\ref{fig:divts}), we excluded years with fewer than six monthly observations, which additionally removed 2016 (n=5). For hierarchical clustering based on presence-absence, we retained 2016 because binary metrics require only one detection per year to record a genus as present, making them relatively robust to missing months. For the NMDS ordination, density distributions, and gradient forest analysis, we used monthly observations directly, including all available data from 2014–2016. For monthly-resolution analyses, missing observations reduce sample size but do not bias the detected relationships, as each month is treated as an independent observation with contemporaneous environmental and biological measurements. Despite gaps in 2014 (3 missing months) and 2015 (4 missing months), both years contained observations from all three seasons (primary upwelling, secondary upwelling, and rainy season), reducing the risk of seasonal bias.
    
        Primary productivity data had additional gaps, with coverage declining markedly after 2009. Given the strong covariance between primary productivity and chlorophyll~\textit{a} ($\rho = 0.70$), we used chlorophyll~\textit{a} as the primary indicator of community productivity while including available PP data for comparison.

\subsection{Interannual changes in the environment and ecosystem}
    % Figure 2
    
    Normalized annual anomalies reveal coordinated interannual changes across environmental variables, chlorophyll~\textit{a}, and phytoplankton cell counts (Figure~\ref{fig:zscore}). Distinct patterns emerge in wind speed, precipitation, SST, and \qty{21}{\celsius} isotherm depth.  Years with positive wind anomalies (red) correspond to negative anomalies in SST and isotherm depth (blue, indicating a shallower isotherm), reflecting stronger upwelling conditions. These patterns are consistent with the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO), where a positive AMO phase is correlated with weaker upwelling regimes (see also Figure~\ref{fig:sup:correlation} in the Appendix). \linelabel{R2:C20} Nitrate and phosphate concentrations show strong positive anomalies at the beginning of the time series, reflecting intensified upwelling that elevates annual mean concentrations. Both nutrients remained relatively low between 2002 and 2014, with nitrate showing greater interannual variability. \linelabel{R1:C14} Silicate anomalies, in contrast, did not follow the same pattern as nitrate and phosphate. Instead, annual anomalies show an inverse relationship with precipitation and salinity, indicating effects of surface runoff, which likely drove the elevated silicate concentrations observed between 2008 and 2013 \cite{lorenzoni_characterization_2015}. Reduced consumption due to lower diatom abundance may have also contributed.
    Chlorophyll \textit{a}, primary productivity, and functional group cell counts show positive anomalies throughout most of the first half of the time series, followed by a significant reduction after 2004, suggesting a shift in community structure.
    \linelabel{R1:C15:start} This biological shift was preceded by a change in the AMO index anomalies that occurred after 2002, when the index transitioned to a period of mostly positive anomalies that persisted until 2013. This period was also characterized by upwelling-related variables indicating a weak upwelling regime (low wind, high SST, deep isotherm). Thus, while the AMO and physical parameters shifted after 2002, the corresponding change in chlorophyll \textit{a} and cell counts occurred later, after 2004. \linelabel{R1:C15:end}
    \linelabel{R2:C15:sparse} Although data are sparse after 2013, with 2016 and 2017 excluded from annual mean calculations due to insufficient monthly coverage, the observations available for 2014 and 2015 show a strong increase in nutrients and biological variables, coinciding with a return of the AMO index to negative anomalies and with stronger upwelling conditions. \linelabel{R2:C15:sparse:end}
    \linelabel{R2:C23:start} Diversity metrics showed different temporal patterns than biomass indicators. \linelabel{R1:C16} Shannon diversity z-scores declined between 1998 and 2007, then increased between 2007 and 2013, during a period when chlorophyll~\textit{a} and cell counts actually declined. This inverse relationship between diversity and biomass is consistent with our understanding of upwelling systems, where strong upwelling favors dominance by fewer specialized taxa such as diatoms (\cite{james_influence_2022}). However, in 1996 and 1997, positive anomalies in cell counts, chlorophyll~\textit{a}, and diversity metrics coincided, suggesting that additional factors other than upwelling influenced community structure during this period. \linelabel{R2:C23:end}

    Figure~\ref{fig:divts} shows the depth distribution of chlorophyll~\textit{a} and diversity indices across seasons. Approximately 80\,\% of chlorophyll~\textit{a} was found in the upper 50 meters of the water column, with a further 15\,\% between 50 and 75 meters. Chlorophyll \textit{a} exhibited marked seasonality, with the highest biomass in the upper water column during the primary upwelling season (December–May, Figure \ref{fig:divts} $b$). The yearly mean chlorophyll \textit{a} biomass showed the highest variability within the upper \qty{25}{meters}. In some years (1999, 2005, 2008, 2009, and 2013), concentrations in this layer were lower than at 25–50~m depth, indicating a deeper chlorophyll maximum (Figure~\ref{fig:divts}$a$). The low value in 2013 partly reflects missing data from January and February (during primary upwelling). However, even the available months showed reduced concentrations (65\,\% of the long-term mean), with a return to higher values in 2014. Chlorophyll \textit{a} biomass below 50 meters depth showed a relatively stable distribution throughout the year and across the time series. During the rainy season, biomass in the upper \qty{25}{\meter} was markedly reduced while the 50–75~m interval showed a slight increase, indicating a deeper chlorophyll maximum under weak upwelling conditions.
    
    \linelabel{R2:C24:start} Genus diversity showed both seasonal and interannual patterns. At the seasonal scale, richness generally followed chlorophyll~\textit{a} dynamics, with maximum values during primary upwelling. At the interannual scale, distinct trends emerged across the time series. 
    Genus richness was highest in the upper \qty{25}{\meter}, which also showed the greatest interannual variability. Richness peaked at the start of the time series (1996–1997), declined across all depths, and increased again after 2011. 
    This decline did not coincide with chlorophyll~\textit{a}, which remained relatively stable until 2004 (Figure~\ref{fig:divts}$a$), suggesting that factors beyond biomass influenced diversity during this period.
    The Shannon index, which scales genus richness by cell count abundances, showed similar interannual patterns but with less pronounced variability. Annual mean Shannon diversity declined after 1997 but remained elevated through 2000 before decreasing further. The index increased again after 2011, though marked reductions in annual means were observed in the upper \qty{25}{meters} in 2006, 2009, and 2015. \linelabel{R2:C21} Notably, the seasonal signal apparent in chlorophyll \textit{a} and  richness was not evident in the Shannon index (see Figure \ref{fig:divts} f). \linelabel{R2:C24:end}

    Community evenness, expressed by the Pielou index, shows no seasonal signal and a very large variance within seasons and through depth (see Figure \ref{fig:divts} $h$). The mean interannual dynamics of evenness were remarkably stable across all depth intervals, clustered around an intermediate 0.5 value. There were marked reductions in all depths towards 2000 and for all depths above 75 meters in 2006, 2009, and 2015. Throughout 2003 to 2013 evenness was almost consistently highest in the deepest interval below 75 meters, where the community appeared to have been more evenly distributed.

\subsection{Clustering analysis yields contrasting ecological regimes}
    % Figure 4
    Hierarchical clustering of annually aggregated presence-absence data revealed distinct community regimes. Figure \ref{fig:clustering} $a$ shows the results of clustering annually aggregated communities using a linear complete linkage algorithm based on the binary Jaccard matrix. Two distinct clusters emerge from this analysis, with the first cluster encompassing the years 1996-2003 and 2014-2016 (hereafter Early and Late Cluster 1, respectively), and the second cluster including the intermediate years from 2004 to 2013 (Cluster 2).
    Interannual genus turnover (Figure~\ref{fig:clustering}$b$) increased from 1996 to 2004, decreased during Cluster~2, and increased again in 2014, marking a transition toward Cluster~1 conditions. This pattern is reflected in the dendrogram, where years within Cluster~2 show smaller distances, indicating more stable community composition.
    Dominant genera differed among periods: the diatom \textit{Thalassiosira} dominated Early Cluster~1 and remained abundant throughout, \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} and the haptophyte \textit{Emiliania} dominated Cluster~2, and \textit{Emiliania} dominated cell counts of Late Cluster~1.
    \linelabel{R2:C25} Note that the hierarchical clustering reflects similarity in taxonomic composition (presence-absence), while the dominant genera reflect abundance structure. These complementary perspectives reveal that, although dominance patterns shifted, the overall taxonomic pool in Late Cluster~1 more closely resembled Early Cluster~1 than Cluster~2.
    
    To check whether the annual aggregation of community data smoothed out strong seasonal signals, we performed an NMDS cluster analysis using the monthly cell counts (Figure \ref{fig:clustering} $c$). The NMDS clustering is overlaid with environmental data and climate indices in an attempt to unveil some of the underlying trends. The horizontal axis separates the two clusters, with cluster 2 located over positive anomalies in AMO and precipitation, and Early Cluster 1 located over positive anomalies in salinity and MEI v.2. Late Cluster 1 shows a transitional state where the majority of monthly community data points align with Early Cluster 1, while some scatter within Cluster 2. The second NMDS axis appears to follow the trends in upwelling intensity, as indicated by the vectors pointing in opposite directions for nutrient concentrations, temperature, and wind speed. Overall, the NMDS ordination confirms the broad pattern shown by clustering the annually aggregated data, although some monthly observations from the 2014-2016 period occupy positions in ordination space within Cluster 2, consistent with a community in transition.

In contrast, SST and salinity display clear recovery patterns, with Late Cluster~1 distributions overlapping substantially with Early Cluster~1 while both differ from Cluster~2. Nitrate and phosphate show partial evidence of recovery: both differed significantly between Early Cluster~1 and Cluster~2, with no difference between the two Cluster~1 periods, but the comparisons between Cluster~2 and Late Cluster~1 did not reach significance ($p_{adj} = 0.13$ and $0.25$, respectively), possibly reflecting limited statistical power for the smaller Late Cluster~1 sample.

However, the comparison between Cluster~2 and Late Cluster~1 was not significant ($p_{adj} = 0.13$), suggesting that chlorophyll had not fully recovered by the end of the time series. In contrast, diatoms and cyanobacteria show incomplete recovery: Late Cluster~1 differs significantly from both Cluster~2 and Early Cluster~1, indicating that these groups had not returned to pre-2004 abundances by the end of the time series. Primary productivity showed no significant differences between any of the cluster periods.
    Diversity metrics showed limited differentiation among clusters. Neither genus richness nor the Shannon index differed significantly between cluster periods. However, Pielou's evenness was significantly higher during Cluster~2 compared to the combined Cluster~1 periods, with the density distribution for Cluster~2 showing a higher median than either Early or Late Cluster~1. This indicates that while total diversity remained stable, the community was more evenly distributed among genera during the low-productivity Cluster~2 period, likely reflecting reduced dominance by typically abundant taxa such as diatoms and haptophytes.

\subsection{AMO and upwelling-related variables drive community changes}
    % Figure 6

    Gradient forest analysis identified the environmental variables most strongly associated with phytoplankton community shifts. Preliminary model runs were made to determine whether time lags of the predictor variables could better explain the data (see Table \ref{tab:sup:GFlagtests} in Appendix), resulting in the selection of the AMO with a lag of 2 years and MEI v.2 with a lag of 4 years. Figure \ref{fig:GF} $a$ shows the predictor variables ranked by their weighted importance, with the AMO and MEI v.2 indices with time lags, as well as upwelling-related variables such as SST and nitrate in the upper 100 meters, demonstrating the highest predictive capacity. Wind speed, precipitation, and phosphate concentration scored the lowest in predicting changes within the community. 
    Gradient forest predicts shifts in the community by building a random forest model for each observed genus across the time series. The predictive power of the models for each genus is shown in Figure \ref{fig:GF} $b$. The microscopy data are biased toward larger cell sizes and, therefore, most of the predicted genus cell counts are diatoms. However, two haptophyte and three dinoflagellate genera rank relatively high. For the presented run, a total of 89 genera (each observed more than 10 times over the time series) were used as indicator variables, of which 46 genera could be predicted using the given predictor variables, which corresponds to more than \qty{95}{\%} of counted cells (see Table \ref{tab:sup:GenusFuncGroups} for a complete list of identified genera). 
    Figure \ref{fig:GF} $c$ shows the cumulative importance of the predictor variables across the parameter range. The dashed black line indicates cumulative importance across the entire community, and the solid colored lines indicate importance per phytoplankton group. 
    \linelabel{R1:C24:start} Cumulative importance curves from gradient forest analysis show how the importance of a predictor accumulates across its range of values. Because these curves are cumulative, they increase monotonically. The slope of the curve at any point indicates where the strongest compositional turnover occurs. A steep slope indicates that small changes in the predictor correspond to large changes in the community composition. A leveling off indicates that the predictor has little influence on the community composition. For the AMO index, we see a clear pattern in which negative anomalies affect the community (slope is highest for negative values). \linelabel{R1:C24:end} This contrasts with a higher importance for positive anomalies in the MEI v.2 index, particularly for haptophytes and cyanobacteria. Diatoms show a strong threshold at extremely shallow isotherm, representing very strong upwelling conditions, while all other functional groups show increase predictive capability towards a deeper isotherm.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Community reorganization and biomass shifts}
    % P1: Introductory paragraph highlighting the overall dynamics/shifts
    This study reveals the long-term dynamics of a tropical coastal marine ecosystem driven by local environmental variability, which is, in turn, influenced by large-scale climatic changes. In the Cariaco Basin, decadal-scale climate oscillations impacted the wind-driven upwelling regime, producing corresponding effects on the composition and biodiversity of the phytoplankton community.
    These changes are clearly visible in the yearly mean anomalies and the density distribution of monthly measurements. After 2004, we observed increased SST, decreased wind speeds, reduced fluorometric chlorophyll \textit{a}, and drastically reduced cell counts of functional groups, all coincident with a shift in the AMO index toward positive mean anomalies until 2014. \linelabel{R2:C26} Comparison of the period following the shift (2004-2013) against the 1996-2003 baseline reveals that average cell counts for diatoms and dinoflagellates were about 10-fold and 6-fold lower, respectively. Cyanobacteria decreased almost 30-fold. Fluorometric chlorophyll \textit{a} concentrations also decreased, but to a lesser extent (less than 2-fold), indicating that microscopy cell counts overestimate the reduction in phytoplankton biomass.
    By considering the phytoplankton community time series up to the end of 2016, we found that the observed shift that occurred in 2004 appears to have produced a temporary regime that lasted only until 2013 (Cluster 2), after which environmental conditions and taxonomic composition showed signs of reverting toward a state more similar to that of 1996-2004 (Early Cluster 1), consistent with the observations by \citeA{muller-karger_scientific_2019}. The year 2004 represents a clear transitional point in the time series: it exhibited the highest interannual genus turnover observed across the entire study period (Figure~\ref{fig:clustering}b), and was the only year whose cluster assignment changed when alternative clustering methods were applied, switching from Cluster~2 to Cluster~1 using Ward's method (Figure~\ref{fig:sup:annual_clust_comp}). In 2014, physical conditions in the Cariaco Basin shifted back toward a regime of increased wind speed, increased upwelling, and elevated nutrient concentrations.
    Concurrently, we observed an increase in chlorophyll \textit{a} and a return to a community composition more similar to the period between 1996 and 2004. 
    \linelabel{R2:C24} This grouping of Late Cluster 1 with Early Cluster 1 reflects similarity in overall taxonomic composition, as measured by presence-absence (binary Jaccard distance), rather than in dominant taxa. The identity of dominant genera differed among periods, shifting from \textit{Thalassiosira}/\textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} in 1996-2003, to \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia}/\textit{Emiliania} in 2004-2013, to \textit{Emiliania}/\textit{Thalassiosira} in 2014-2016, yet the clustering analysis indicates that the broader taxonomic pool present in Late Cluster~1 more closely resembled Early Cluster~1 than the intermediate period.

    % P2: Discuss community reorganisation, biomass shifts vs HPLC data 
    When comparing the clusters, we observed a marked reduction in cell counts, which corresponded to a decreased concentration of chlorophyll \textit{a}, particularly in the top \qty{25}{meters} of the water column (see Figure \ref{fig:divts}). The decline in cell counts was not proportional to the more modest decrease in total chlorophyll \textit{a}, suggesting that the phytoplankton community might have transitioned to smaller cells, which were probably below the detection size limit of optical microscopes \cite{muller-karger_scientific_2019}. \linelabel{R2:C28} This shift to smaller cell sizes and a reduction in large diatoms is consistent with predicted effects of warming and reduced mixing on phytoplankton communities \cite{bopp_response_2005}. Increases in temperature can lead to a decrease in the relative contribution of larger cells in the community, irrespective of nutrient availability, but shifts towards smaller cells are particularly prevalent when temperatures increase and nutrient supply decreases \cite{acevedo-trejos_glimpse_2014, mousing_global_2014}, as we observed in the Cariaco Basin. During the 2014-2016 period, cell counts of diatoms and dinoflagellates increased relative to Cluster~2, and chlorophyll \textit{a} concentrations showed signs of recovery, suggesting a partial reversal of the shift toward smaller cells as upwelling conditions strengthened. However, the magnitude of this recovery varied among functional groups, and cell counts did not return to the levels observed during Early Cluster~1, indicating that the community remained in a transitional state at the end of the time series. Furthermore, sparse data coverage after 2013 and the abrupt termination of the CARIACO program in 2017 limit our ability to determine whether this transition would have continued toward a full recovery. 

    Comparison with High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data from the Cariaco Basin \cite{pinckney_phytoplankton_2015} revealed both similarities and differences with our results. While general patterns aligned, the HPLC data showed an increase in chlorophyll \textit{a} between 1996-2000 and 2006-2010, particularly at depths below \qty{55}{\meter}. However, the HPLC data were analyzed by multiple laboratories with a gap in coverage between 2000 and 2006, and total chlorophyll \textit{a} could not be quantified consistently across laboratories \cite{pinckney_phytoplankton_2015}. For this reason, we focused our analysis on the more temporally complete microscopy cell count and fluorometric chlorophyll \textit{a} data. Our results are corroborated by independent spectrophotometric measurements at \qty{20}{\meter} depth off Margarita Island, which similarly showed reduced chlorophyll concentrations between 2004 and 2014, and a subsequent increase in 2016 and 2017 \cite{gomez_gaspar_variacion_2025}. The shift toward smaller cells is further supported by the HPLC data, which indicated an increase in non-diatom taxa during the 2006-2010 period \cite{pinckney_phytoplankton_2015}.
    \linelabel{R2:C14} Our depth-resolved analysis also suggested a deepening of the chlorophyll maximum during periods of reduced upwelling, in accordance with findings from HPLC data \cite{pinckney_phytoplankton_2015}. This vertical redistribution is likely linked to changes in euphotic zone depth, documented as \qtyrange{60}{75}{\meter} during stratified, low-production periods compared to \qtyrange{15}{30}{\meter} during upwelling \cite{lorenzoni_characterization_2015, pinckney_phytoplankton_2015}. Unfortunately, PAR data were not collected during the CARIACO time series and euphotic depth measurements are only available until 2012, precluding direct analysis of light-driven vertical distribution changes across the full study period.


    % P3: Shift in Diversity does not match shift in biomass/cell counts, why?
    The temporal dynamics of yearly mean genus richness did not correspond directly to the observed clusters. Instead, richness declined markedly from 1998 to 2009, then increased again starting around 2010, preceding the environmental shift that marked the onset of Late Cluster~1 by approximately four years. Shannon diversity and Pielou's evenness showed similar trends, albeit less pronounced, with reduced yearly means between 2000 and 2006 coinciding with increased nanoflagellate counts and a pronounced peak in cyanobacteria.

    Despite these temporal trends, we did not find significant differences in genus richness or Shannon diversity between the clusters. However, we did observe a significant difference in Pielou's evenness, with the phytoplankton community becoming more evenly distributed during the low upwelling conditions of Cluster~2. This finding is consistent with the analysis of \citeA{pinckney_phytoplankton_2015} based on HPLC pigment data. The increase in evenness is most likely explained by the lack of strong upwelling events and the resulting reduced disturbance of the community. \linelabel{R1:C30} Under strong upwelling conditions, r-selected species are typically favored, leading to dominance by fewer taxa and consequently lower evenness. A corresponding decrease in compositional turnover was also observed during Cluster~2 (see Figure \ref{fig:clustering}$b$). As the productivity and biomass of phytoplankton communities are driven primarily by nutrients supplied through disturbance events, this pattern aligns with the inverse relationship between evenness and community performance observed in previous studies \cite{lehtinen_phytoplankton_2017, otero_phytoplankton_2020}.

    
    
\subsection{Drivers of change}
    % P1: Introduce GF Analysis and key findings
    To identify the variables that best predict changes in the phytoplankton community, we conducted a gradient forest analysis using time series of climate indices and in situ measurements to predict genus-level abundances from microscopy cell counts. Gradient forest is an extension of the random forest algorithm, designed to identify thresholds and patterns in community composition along environmental gradients \cite{ellis_gradient_2012}. Limiting the analysis to bottom-up drivers ensured the greatest possible data coverage throughout the CARIACO time series. Our analysis revealed that the strongest predictor of change within the community was not an in situ measurement but rather the AMO index, followed by nitrate concentration, SST, and the MEI v.2 (see Figure \ref{fig:GF}$a$). In addition to ranking predictor importance, the gradient forest analysis identifies threshold responses from cumulative importance curves (Figure \ref{fig:GF}$c$), which indicate the ranges of predictor values where the strongest shifts in community composition occur.
    
    % P2: AMO - mechanism and thresholds
    Although regional effects may vary, a negative phase in the AMO is generally associated with periods of colder surface temperatures and a southward shift of the ITCZ \cite{knight_climate_2006, colna_latitudinal_2017}. In the Cariaco Basin, a southward shift of the ITCZ correlates with increased wind-driven upwelling \cite{taylor_ecosystem_2012}. The threshold responses for the AMO showed contrasting patterns among functional groups. Diatoms, dinoflagellates, and haptophytes exhibited strong response thresholds for negative AMO anomalies, indicating the influence of stronger upwelling conditions on these groups. Haptophytes additionally showed a threshold effect at positive AMO anomalies, which may be related to the pattern observed by \citeA{pinckney_phytoplankton_2015} based on HPLC data showing haptophytes blooming more frequently during 2006 to 2010, a period characterized by mostly positive AMO anomalies.

    % P3: ENSO - mechanism
    The El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO) also exhibited strong predictive power for community composition, ranking fourth after AMO, nitrate concentration and SST. ENSO exhibits a strong teleconnection to the Caribbean Sea: during El Niño events (indicated by a positive MEI v.2 index), sea surface temperatures rise and trade winds blow more to the north-northwest \cite{enfield_tropical_1997}, implying a general reduction in upwelling strength. Such a reduction was indeed observed in the Cariaco Basin during the 1997 to 1998 El Niño \cite{romero_seasonal_2009}. However, the mechanistic link between ENSO and the Cariaco ecosystem is not straightforward. The MEI v.2 is significantly positively correlated with local salinity and negatively correlated with the AMO index, but it is not significantly correlated with local upwelling-related variables (see Figure \ref{fig:sup:correlation} in Appendix). While trade wind strength and SST are generally anti-correlated in the Caribbean Sea, positive trade wind anomalies have been observed during El Niño conditions and the reverse during La Niña \cite{chang_coupled_2013}. \citeA{taylor_ecosystem_2012} found no strong correlation between MEI v.2 and other variables in the Cariaco Basin, with only \qtyrange{24}{36}{\percent} of variance explained using linear regression with a 12-month time lag. In contrast, we found relatively strong predictive power for changes in the phytoplankton community using the MEI v.2 index with a 4-year time lag (Table \ref{tab:sup:GFlagtests} in Appendix).
    
    % P4: ENSO - thresholds and secondary upwelling
    The threshold responses for MEI v.2 revealed functional group-specific patterns. Dinoflagellate abundances shifted most strongly at negative MEI v.2 anomalies, representing La Niña conditions, consistent with Bayesian modeling findings showing that dinoflagellates responded positively to warmer, stratified conditions \cite{mutshinda_environmental_2013}. Cyanobacteria and haptophytes exhibited pronounced threshold responses to positive MEI v.2 anomalies, demonstrating sensitivity to El Niño events. Diatoms did not exhibit a clear threshold response but showed slightly stronger responses in the positive range of the MEI v.2. Interestingly, positive MEI v.2 anomalies, representing strong ENSO events, occurred predominantly during the high upwelling regime of Cluster~1 (see Figure \ref{fig:clustering}$c$). This pattern could be explained by findings that strong El Niño events increase the strength of currents in the Caribbean Sea through modulating easterly wind anomalies, thereby potentially enhancing upwelling strength in the Cariaco Basin \cite{huang_enso_2023}. In particular, this intensification of the Caribbean Current may enhance the secondary upwelling that typically occurs between June and August \cite{rueda-roa_description_2018}, rather than affecting the primary wind-driven upwelling between December and May. \linelabel{R1:C31} Additionally, enhanced eddy activity near the Cariaco Basin during strong El Niño events can stimulate productivity and alter community composition through ventilation of sub-oxic waters below the sill \cite{astor_seasonal_2003}.
    
    % P5: In-situ measurements
    The in situ measurements that best predicted changes in the microphytoplankton community were nitrate concentration, SST, and salinity, in that order. Haptophytes and diatoms exhibited steep thresholds around \qtyrange{22}{24}{\celsius}, indicating strong effects on cell counts at low surface temperatures indicative of strong upwelling. Dinoflagellates showed a threshold at higher temperatures, around \qty{26}{\celsius}, corresponding to their ecological preference for stratified conditions. Nitrate is a crucial nutrient for phytoplankton growth and has been identified as the growth-limiting nutrient during stratification periods in the Cariaco Basin \cite{muller-karger_scientific_2019}. For diatoms, a strong threshold response was observed above \qty{7}{\micro M}, indicating that intense upwelling conditions drove growth in large diatoms, resulting in predictable changes in cell counts.

    % P6: Seasonal GF analysis
    To assess whether dominant predictors differed among seasons, we conducted exploratory gradient forest analyses separately for primary upwelling, secondary upwelling, and rainy season observations (Figure~\ref{fig:sup:GF_seasonal}). Despite reduced sample sizes for secondary upwelling ($n = 47$) and rainy season ($n = 42$), the AMO consistently ranked among the top predictors across all seasons. For primary upwelling, the MEI v.2 ranked first, followed closely by AMO, suggesting that ENSO teleconnections are particularly relevant during this period. During secondary upwelling, silicate and precipitation emerged among the top predictors alongside AMO, consistent with the influence of freshwater runoff during the June to August period when precipitation increases. The composition of predictable genera also varied by season: primary upwelling predicted predominantly diatoms with contributions from dinoflagellates, haptophytes, and cyanobacteria, whereas secondary upwelling and rainy season predictions were dominated by diatoms and dinoflagellates, with dinoflagellates particularly prominent during the rainy season. These seasonal patterns reinforce the conclusion that climate indices, especially the AMO, exert year-round influence on community composition, while the relative importance of other predictors varies with seasonal oceanographic conditions.

    % P6: NMDS synthesis
    These results are consistent with the NMDS ordination of monthly cell counts (Figure \ref{fig:clustering}$c$). The axis that separated the two clusters (NMDS 1) co-varied most strongly with AMO, MEI v.2, salinity, and precipitation. Variables directly related to upwelling conditions, such as temperature, \qty{21}{\celsius} isotherm depth, wind speed, and nutrient concentrations, varied more strongly along NMDS 2. Salinity and silicate concentration, which depend on both water mass exchanges through upwelling and freshwater input from precipitation and surface runoff, showed greater variance along NMDS 1. The alignment of salinity with the MEI v.2 is consistent with the influence of ENSO on regional hydrology: El Niño events typically result in reduced precipitation and drier conditions in the southern Caribbean, whereas La Niña years are associated with higher precipitation, leading to elevated silicate concentrations and lower surface salinity \cite{giannini_interdecadal_2001}. Salinity and silicate concentration exhibited some explanatory power in the gradient forest analysis, although they were weaker predictors than SST, nitrate concentration, and climate indices (see Figure \ref{fig:GF}).

    

\subsection{Limitations}
    % P1: Discussion of missing data and temporal aggregation
    \linelabel{R2:missing-discussion} Our analyses employed different temporal aggregations depending on the research question. Annual aggregation for z-scores and hierarchical clustering was chosen to emphasize interannual trends and reduce the influence of seasonal variability and extreme monthly values on long-term pattern detection. However, annual averaging may mask important sub-annual dynamics, particularly given the strong seasonality of the Cariaco upwelling system. To address this concern, we complemented annually aggregated analyses with monthly-resolution approaches (NMDS ordination, gradient forest analysis) that preserve seasonal signals.

    Several lines of evidence indicate that interannual variability, rather than seasonality, represents the dominant mode of community change. PERMANOVA analysis showed that temporal clusters explained approximately five times more variance in community composition than season ($R^2 = 17.9\%$ vs.\ $3.5\%$, both $p = 0.001$, Table~\ref{tab:sup:permanova_season_cluster}), and NMDS ordination of monthly community data showed no clear separation among seasons (Figure~\ref{fig:sup:nmds_season}). Furthermore, exploratory gradient forest analyses conducted separately for each season confirmed that the AMO consistently ranked among the top predictors across primary upwelling, secondary upwelling, and rainy season periods (Figure~\ref{fig:sup:GF_seasonal}). These results support our use of annually aggregated analyses to characterize the temporal regimes..
    
    A further limitation concerns the interpretation of community dynamics during the final years of the time series. Monthly sampling coverage declined after 2013, with 2016 and 2017 excluded from annual mean calculations due to insufficient observations. Despite these gaps, the available data from 2014 to 2016 provide evidence of a shift in environmental conditions back toward a stronger upwelling regime, and the taxonomic composition during this period clusters with Early Cluster~1 based on presence-absence similarity. While the sparse coverage limits our ability to fully characterize the trajectory of community change during these years, the concordance between environmental indicators and community composition supports the interpretation of a system in transition. The termination of the CARIACO program in 2017 leaves open the question of whether this transition would have culminated in a complete return to the pre-2004 community state.


    % P2: Discuss Limitations of Light microscopy cell counts in CARIACO Time Series
    \linelabel{R2:C31} Using microscopy cell counts as a basis for our analysis allowed us to focus on changes in the microphytoplankton community. Our choice of grouping cell counts by genus was based on the assumption that species within a genus generally exhibit similar eco-physiological responses. However, this approach does not capture the complete phytoplankton community of the Cariaco Basin. The HPLC pigment data used by \citeauthor{pinckney_phytoplankton_2015} captured all size classes of phytoplankton and showed less drastic declines in diatoms and even slight increases in other functional groups after 2004. With the caveat that the microscopy counts captured microphytoplankton almost exclusively, this discrepancy can be explained: drastic declines in cell counts reflected shifts to smaller species, an aspect consistent with size spectra calculated by previous studies using HPLC pigment data \cite{lorenzoni_characterization_2015}. \linelabel{R2:C8:2} Comparative studies have shown that microscopic estimates become less consistent when small cells dominate the community and that diatom contributions to total biomass tend to be higher when estimated by microscopy compared to pigment-based methods \cite{schluter_use_2000, havskum_routine_2004}. Such methodological differences would clearly also impact the interpretation of environmental effects on the community.

    A specific example of these limitations is provided by \textit{Synechococcus}, which was detected exclusively during Cluster~1 (both early and late periods), with zero counts recorded throughout the entire Cluster~2 period. This pattern is opposite to ecological expectations, as \textit{Synechococcus} typically thrives under stratified, oligotrophic conditions that characterized Cluster~2. Rather than reflecting true absence, this likely reflects the limitations of light microscopy: \textit{Synechococcus} cells are approximately \qty{1}{\micro\meter} in diameter, well below the approximately \qty{20}{\micro\meter} detection limit of the Utermöhl method. The \textit{Synechococcus} cells detected during Cluster~1 were likely large colonial aggregates associated with detrital particles that became visible during high-biomass conditions. Fully resolving the dynamics of cyanobacteria communities, including \textit{Trichodesmium} and \textit{Synechococcus}, in response to stratification and AMO variability would require higher-resolution methods such as flow cytometry or molecular approaches and represents an important avenue for future research in the Cariaco Basin.

    Despite these limitations, the HPLC dataset had limited temporal coverage and was assembled from four different laboratories, introducing potential inconsistencies. We therefore focused on the microscopy data, which provided continuous genus- and species-level observations across the full time series, while acknowledging that our analysis may over- or underestimate the magnitude of community change when shifts occur toward or among smaller size fractions.

    % P3: Discuss limitations of GF analysis & Limitation to bottom-up drivers and climate indices
    The limited scope of the microscopy cell counts also constrained the gradient forest analysis to microphytoplankton genera. 
    Another potential caveat is that many of the predictors in ecological time series are correlated (Figure~\ref{fig:sup:correlation} in Appendix), which can lead to the identification of a correlated predictor that has no direct effect on community changes. The gradient forest methodology partly mitigates this problem by conditionally permuting subsets of correlated predictors \cite{ellis_gradient_2012}. Additionally, random forest models do not consider temporal relationships between data points, which is why we tested for predictive relationships with time lags. Without adjusting for time lags, SST and nitrate concentration are the strongest predictors of community change (Figure~\ref{fig:sup:GFoutput_nolags} in Appendix). Incorporating time lags slightly improves the overall predictive strength, as quantified by the $R^2$ weighted importance, although both SST and the AMO index yielded relatively low values around 0.03. This is expected given the noisy nature of phytoplankton microscopy cell counts and is consistent with values reported in other gradient forest studies \cite{pitcher_exploring_2012, roubeix_identification_2016, samhouri_defining_2017, fraker_temporal_2022}. It is important to note that predictive relationships do not necessarily imply mechanistic causal relationships. The scope of our analysis, like any statistical analysis, is exploratory and aims to narrow down potential key relationships. Mathematical modeling and experimental work are required to develop a clearer mechanistic understanding of the drivers of ecosystem change in the Cariaco Basin.

    Our focus on bottom-up drivers of community change may have restricted the explanatory power of our analysis, as top-down processes such as zooplankton grazing may have impacted phytoplankton community structure \cite{frank_ups_2007, banas_adding_2011, acevedo-trejos_mechanisms_2015}. \linelabel{R1:C34} For instance, a trophic cascade resulting from the collapse of sardine fisheries and a subsequent increase in mesozooplankton abundance was proposed to have affected the phytoplankton community in the Cariaco Basin \cite{rueda-roa_spatial_2017, muller-karger_scientific_2019}. However, this predictor could not be included in our gradient forest analysis due to a lack of data at monthly resolution. Given the scarcity of data covering higher trophic levels and the absence of zooplankton data for the first six years of the time series, mechanistic modeling would be the ideal approach for investigating the relative impacts of top-down and bottom-up drivers.


    

\section{Conclusions}

    We found that decadal-scale climate variability, particularly linked to the AMO index, strongly influenced phytoplankton community composition in the tropical coastal ecosystem of the Cariaco Basin. Phytoplankton cell counts revealed two distinct community regimes: the first, observed during 1996 to 2003 (Early Cluster~1), was associated with strong upwelling conditions and negative AMO anomalies. A shift to the second regime occurred after 2004 (Cluster~2), characterized by weak upwelling and positive AMO anomalies. While overall diversity remained similar between regimes, the low-upwelling period showed reduced taxonomic turnover and increased evenness. These shifts did not reflect a permanent regime change. In 2014, environmental conditions reverted toward a stronger upwelling regime with negative AMO anomalies, and the community began transitioning toward a composition more similar to the early period (Late Cluster~1). This transition occurred despite multi-year trends of surface warming and steep reductions in microphytoplankton abundance during Cluster~2 (\cite{taylor_ecosystem_2012}), suggesting a degree of resilience in the phytoplankton community to climate-driven perturbations.

    Multiple lines of evidence support the interpretation of a system in transition toward pre-2004 conditions. Hierarchical clustering grouped Late Cluster~1 with Early Cluster~1 based on taxonomic composition, while NMDS ordination revealed an intermediate pattern, with most Late Cluster~1 observations aligning with Early Cluster~1 but some remaining within the Cluster~2 ordination space. Several environmental variables (e.g., SST and salinity) and functional groups (haptophytes and dinoflagellates) showed statistically similar distributions between Early and Late Cluster~1 while differing significantly from Cluster~2. Other variables, notably diatom and cyanobacteria abundances, had not fully returned to Early Cluster~1 levels. Together, these patterns indicate that the community was in a transitional state more similar to the pre-2004 regime at the end of the time series. The sparse data coverage after 2013 and the termination of the CARIACO program in 2017 preclude definitive conclusions about whether this transition would have culminated in a complete return.
    
    Gradient forest analysis identified the AMO as the strongest predictor of community change, followed by nitrate concentration, SST, and the MEI v.2, indicating that both Atlantic and Pacific climate oscillations influence the Cariaco ecosystem through their effects on upwelling dynamics. Our findings highlight the importance of long-term ecological time series for capturing non-linear ecosystem responses and underscore the potential of the AMO as a useful indicator of ecosystem state in this region, consistent with observations by \citeA{nye_ecosystem_2014}. Although our study is subject to limitations, particularly regarding microscopy detection of small phytoplankton and sparse sampling after 2013, it provides evidence of a transition toward earlier ecosystem conditions driven by decadal-scale climate variability. Future work, particularly using mechanistic modeling and higher-resolution taxonomic methods, could help disentangle the relative impacts of bottom-up versus top-down drivers and better resolve the dynamics of smaller phytoplankton size fractions.
